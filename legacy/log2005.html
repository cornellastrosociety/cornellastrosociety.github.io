Cornell Astronomical Society

What we saw, 2005-2006

All observations were made through the 12" refractor unless otherwise specified.

Wednesday, August 16, 2006

Art opened for a few family members in town on vacation. He showed the usual objects: Jupiter, Albireo (a colorful double star), M13 (Hercules Cluster; in both binoculars and the telescope), the Coathanger (a.k.a. Al Sufi's Cluster or Brocchi's Cluster), and a very bright (magnitude -6) Iridium flare. One person stayed longer, and Art showed him the spiral galaxy NGC 7331 and (with assistance from Jaghadeep and Mike) Uranus. Art left the two looking for a faint galaxies. After failing to find NGC 6503 and NGC 6482, Mike decided to point to the much more familiar M27 (Dumbbell Nebula) and captured a very promising image with his camera.

Tuesday, August 15, 2006

A last quarter moon and mostly clear skies allowed for some challenging star hops as we attempted to view uncommon and lesser known NGC objects. These included the planetary nebulae NGC 6210, NGC 7008, NGC 7026, NGC 7027, and NGC 6741 (Phantom Streak Nebula), the open star clusters NGC 7142 and NGC 7086, the spiral galaxy NGC 7606, and Uranus.

Saturday, August 12, 2006

Mike started with some afternoon solar observing. Two prominent sunspots were viewed and photographed along with three solar prominences. Later, in the evening, Mike and Art celebrated the peak of the Perseid meteor shower by enjoying a screening of "Clash of the Titans" projected upon the outer wall of the Johnson art museum. The outdoor showing allowed a lucky few to actually see Perseids while viewing the hero's tale. Following the film, Mike returned to Fuertes to find it empty despite plans for a group observing session. About 8 visitors arrived and requested nebulae, which were happily provided. Shianne stopped in for a moment (apparently taking a break from her moving), asked if anyone else would be coming, discovered it would be only her and Mike, and promptly decided to go back to moving. Mike carried on entertaining the visitors, two of whom stayed to 3:00 AM discussing classical music and viewing over 30 Perseids while stretched out on comforters on the deck. The company they provided was very pleasant and much appreciated. Objects viewed included M57 (Ring Nebula), M26 (an open star cluster), NGC 6905 (Blue Flash Nebula), and a waning gibbous Moon.

Friday, August 11, 2006

A steady stream of visitors showed up (about 50 in all), but the crowds were not overwhelming like in the previous week. It was an excellent night for seeing except for a nearly full moon. Art avoided our usual Jupiter holdup by moving immediately to the Rocking Horse Cluster (NGC 6910). We also saw Albireo, M3 (a globular star cluster), NGC 457 (Owl/ET/Dragonfly Cluster), and NGC 7331 (a galaxy unfortunately washed out by the Moon). The first cold night of the season chased Shianne, Mike, and Jagadheep downstairs. Reportedly there were some juggling lessons involved.

Wednesday, August 9, 2006

Art returned to catch a few things missed in yesterday's moonlight. Got M27 (Dumbbell Nebula), M71, NGC 7789 (a rich star cluster), and NGC 6934 (a globular star cluster). He looked a fairly long time for the globular cluster NGC 7006 but failed to find it; the spiral galaxy NGC 6946 in Cepheus was a very iffy possible catch, but the Moon was up by then. Both this night and the previous night had satellites zip thru the finder scope's viewfield, a somewhat "eep!" experience. Closed at 10:30pm.

Tuesday, August 8, 2006

Art practiced solo until midnight. Cloudless, cool, dry evening; would have been perfect had not a full moon been washing out most things. Started with a fast peek at Jupiter (solely to check the amount of atmospheric turbulence!) then a bunch of open star clusters: NGC 6910 (Rocking Horse), NGC 457 (Owl/E.T./Dragonfly), M52, M103, Do10, NGC 663, and NGC 659 (very faint and tiny). Also got the Blinking Planetary Nebula (NGC 6826), the spiral galaxy NGC 7331, and a quickie practice find of M57 (Ring Nebula). In the binocs M31 (Andromeda Galaxy) was as usual, but M24 (Small Saggitarius Star Cloud) was nearly washed out.

Saturday, August 5, 2006

Brandon brought his Celestron 8-inch Goto telescope, through which we looked at various objects (without keeping a log). Fixed the main telescope's Telrad problem with double-sided tape, then looked at Jupiter (for four visitors), M5 (a globular star cluster), NGC 6891 (star-like planetary nebula), NGC 7217, NGC 7457 (a galaxy), NGC 7006 (globular cluster), NGC 559, NGC 663 (two open star clusters), NGC 6905 (Blue Flash Nebula), and NGC 6934. NGC 6905 and 6934 will make good Friday night targets.

Friday, August 4, 2006

Waxing gibbous moon and a few stray clouds. The first six visitors asked to see the Moon and Jupiter since there was nothing else visible; but before we could get them finished and switch objects, a continuous stream of people began coming up the stairs and we couldn't move to something else until after 10:30--lost count of number of visitors past 90. (A few visitors complained that they'd already seen Jupiter.) Eventual tally of objects: Jupiter, Moon, M11, NGC 7331 (a spiral galaxy), M15 (a globular star cluster), M52, M31 (Andromeda Galaxy), M32 (satellite to M31), NGC xyz [sic], and NGC 891 (an edge-on spiral galaxy, seen only by Mike).

Tuesday, August 1, 2006

Hot clear start to the month with a high temp. of 94 F and a heat index of 105. Brian Kent opened for another CAU session and Mike joined him to host another group of summer students. Viewed the Moon, Jupiter, M3 (a globular star cluster), M13 (Hercules Cluster), M11 (Wild Duck Cluster), NGC 7331, and attempted some photography of M11.

It also came to our attention that the Fuertes observatory can use an ice cream parlor, swimming pool, and water slide extending from the top of the ladder, so visitors can look at Jupiter and simply slide down for a refreshing exit.

Monday, July 31, 2006

Clear but sticky summer night. Art arrived for a little bit of practice and Mike came by after a movie. Sights included a nearly first-quarter waxing crescent Moon, Jupiter, M57 (Ring Nebula), M39 (an open star cluster), M15 (a globular star cluster), and some very impressive meteors (Delta Aquarids?). Mike attempted some astrophotography with the aid of a new T-mount for his digital camera.

Friday, July 28, 2006

Mostly clear public night. Shianne opened, with Mike coming by after ushering for Cornell Cinema. Then, to everyone's amazement, the long-lost Pon made an appearance, quelling rumors of his waning interest in clear Friday nights. We saw M13, M3, Blinking Eye [sic], Jupiter, the Moon, and M57.

Monday, July 24, 2006

Art opened for a small but cosmopolitan group of 4 (Toronto; Edinburgh). We only looked at Jupiter, M4 (a globular star cluster), M51 (Whirlpool Galaxy), and the Coathanger (through binoculars) before the kids in the party got too sleepy. However, about 10pm we did see a cluster of satellites zipping by in multiple directions, including a Truly Splendid™ Iridium flare of magnitude -8(!). (We were 1.6 km from the center, according to heavens-above.com...)

Meanwhile, Mike and Jagadheep escaped the light-polluted haze of Ithaca and fled to the dark sky sanctuary that is Southern Cayuga Central School's observatory. There, Alan Ominsky and his Meade LX90 and LX200 provided a wonderful night of observations, many of which could never be made from the Fuertes. Mike and Jagadheep enjoyed M107, M21, NGC 6546, NGC 6451, NGC 6441, NGC 6400, NGC 6818, M57, M75, M55, NGC 7009, NGC 7293, NGC 6755, M106, M63, NGC 5033, NGC 5005, NGC 6210, M13, M92, M30, M7, M16, M8, M51, Jupiter, M11(!!!), M27, NGC 40, the Blinking Planetary Nebula, M97, the Veil Nebula, and M110, while Shianne studied physics.

Friday, July 21, 2006

A fairly ordinary Friday night viewing session (highlighted by the presence of CAS alum Tanja Bode, in for a visit). We had clouds. We had haze. We had requests for Jupiter. There were several very enthusiastic guests, especially right at the end. We only looked at Jupiter, Albireo (colorful double star), M3 (a globular star cluster), and the Blinking Planetary Nebula.

Wednesday, July 19, 2006

Mike and Art arrived ca. 10:30pm after a movie, with Jagadheep coming by soon after. Art practiced sundry Sagittarius objects for the entertainment of the others (in Jagadheep's words, "You can go for anything you like so long as you do not bore us"): globular star clusters M69, M70, M54 (good tight one, although could not resolve individual stars), M28, M22, and NGC 6638, then the open star cluster M25. We also caught M13 (Hercules Cluster), M31 (Andromeda Galaxy), the Perseus Double Cluster, and the Kemple's Cascade and Coathanger asterisms in binoculars. At 1am Art left Mike Neptune-hunting; after the fact, it is not clear whether he and Jagadheep actually found it.

Tuesday, July 18, 2006

Brian Kent held another (short) observing session for about 40 people, doing only Jupiter. Mike then showed up ca. 11pm to resume his survey of southern galactic clusters, this time including "the impressive" M11 (Wild Duck Cluster), M12, M10, M14, M5, M9, and "the amazingly pathetic" M107. As he was closing ca. 2am, he was massively traumatized by a spider, to the extent of (as he reported the next night) being unable to sleep at all...

Monday, July 17, 2006

A warm, moonless, slightly hazy but mostly clear night provided observing for Mike, special Cornell Adult University guests and summer co-worker Brian. Objects enjoyed included M13 (Hercules Cluster), ε Lyrae (a quadruple star system), Jupiter, M57, M22, M28, M25 (three globular star clusters), M8 (Lagoon Nebula), M21 (an open star cluster), M20 (Trifid Nebula), and "the incomparable" M11.

Saturday, July 15, 2006

After watching V for Vendetta, Shianne, Jagadheep, and Mike arrived at midnight and "rocked out until after 3." In between singing and bouts of boogie-ing they found M16, M17, M18 (three open star clusters), M10, M14, and M2 (three globular star clusters).

Friday, July 14, 2006

The skies were overcast with cirrus at 9pm, but super-optimist Shianne predicted clearing skies; displaying hitherto-unknown powers of weather control, she did indeed clear the sky for several hours, allowing a large crowd to see Jupiter. High clouds precluded observations of most deep-sky objects, but M57 (Ring Nebula) and the Blinking Planetary Nebula were found before it clouded up at 11:30pm.

Thursday, July 13, 2006

Art and Brian Kent (Astro grad doing CAU sessions) opened, this time letting the Taiwanese group have first dibs at seeing things. Due to the crowding on Tuesday, Mike and Art put the C-14 out on the deck and dedicated it to Jupiter-watching so as to leave the main scope available for other things, including M5 (a globular star cluster), Albireo (colorful double star), the Ring Nebula, M51 (Whirlpool Galaxy), and the Blinking Planetary Nebula. After bopping off for a movie, Mike returned to observe solo until 3am. He showed a late group of visitors M13 (Hercules Cluster) and M57, then went to explore the Cygnus starfields. He visited M29, M39, IC 5146, and many other open star clusters. He also spent "too much time" looking for NGC 188, located at +85° [this is extremely close to the north pole, a region of the sky where our telescope is very difficult to point].

Tuesday, July 11, 2006

Due to a miscommunication, there were two groups coming in for a weekday open house, for a total of ca. 75 people, of all ages. As it was, the clouds shut us down by 9:45pm, so that we couldn't even get all of the Cornell Adult University (CAU) group through a Jupiter viewing, let alone any of the second group, some middle-schoolers from Taiwan. (CAU had been scheduled earlier, so they got first dibs on the scope.)

Saturday, July 8, 2006

Hazy evening was extremely quiet (with exception of Classic Rock music compromise) until large group of about 25 students from the h.s. program arrived following the clouds. Closed by 11 after giving the "cloudy night" tour. In our spare time we admired bands of cirrocumulus clouds drifting across the waxing gibbous moon.

Friday, July 7, 2006

Some 60-70 customers early in evening, including multiple families; ca. 12 more arrived at closing. Seeing was bad--another so-called "clear" night, only about 20 stars visible. (Supposedly, particulate haze from Western Canadian forest fires got into the jet stream and is being deposited here...) Saw Moon, Jupiter, Albireo, M57, ε Lyrae, M5. (Hearing also declined precipitously, after a perfectly reasonable start by Samite of Uganda... :-)

Friday, June 30, 2006

Good crowd--60 in early evening, about 10 late. Seeing fair-to-hazy, some clouds. Moon in reg./wide-angle eyepieces, Jupiter (twice), ε Lyrae, M13 in both scope/binocs, M57, M51/NGC 5195, Coathanger cluster in binocs. Ca. 12:45, Mike started a run for M39 & the Cocoon Nebula, only to face an emergency shutdown due to rain. (Of course it immediately cleared up once we'd closed...)

Saturday, June 24, 2006

Customers: six students from the h.s. summer program plus an enthusiastic Vietnamese gentleman. Jupiter, M13, ε Lyrae, M4, M80, M51/NGC 5195. Attempts to view other Sagittarius/Scorpius objects foiled by clouds, which shut us down at 12:20. Very hazy; seeing bad all evening.

Saturday, June 17, 2006

Mercury, Saturn, M94, NGCs 4490, 6826, 6210. A few visitors stopped by. Shianne, Mike, and Jagadheep held wide-ranging discussions on arthropods, et al., especially spiders.

Friday, June 16, 2006

Huge crowd tonight, ~60. (To watch 4 planets?) Set up C-14. Objects looked [at]: Mercury, Mars, Saturn, Jupiter, usual summer suspects (M13, M57, Albireo), M51; M4 through C-14.

Wednesday, June 14, 2006

After a thoroughly humiliating failure to find Mercury in the scope the previous night and annoyed at the waste of an evening, Art returned armed with coordinates for the thing and had it dialed in before sunset. Fulfilled his oath to see all four planets in the scope in one evening and closed at 9:45. In the 12mm eyepiece Mercury was an obvious but featureless half-moon. Mars showed as much disk as Jupiter shows these days in the 10x binocs--might have seen a hint of polar cap, but more likely it was chromatic aberration. Saturn was way too blurred by atmosphere to see the Cassini division in the rings; could barely make out hints of cloud bands. Seeing was excellent for Jupiter in the 32mm, however: lots of structure visible in the equatorial bands and to their sides.

Saturday, June 10, 2006

Finally a clear day, if cold and windy. Art arrived at 6pm to do solar observing (and to find a parking spot, given the glut of alumni cars!). One good sunspot, two prominences; four people stopped by and saw the sun. The evening went very well: a good 60-70 people during the evening (including a number of alums who'd never been to Fuertes while students and were dismayed by what they'd missed, plus one CAS member from the late '70s); it was very crowded at times. Full moon washed out most stuff. Mostly Jupiter for the crowds; some Saturn, but it's now too low in the west for extended viewing. Did get to M13, M44 in binocs, Albireo, M57, and the double-double star ε Lyrae. Shianne put the moon filter on the wide-angle 40mm and we looked a bit at the Moon. Jagadheep convinced Art to try a star-hop from Albireo to M27; he got it just as clouds closed us down at 12:45.

Friday, June 9, 2006

Twelve people came by--pretty good considering it was raining!! It was Alumni Reunion Weekend; public viewing had been listed on the schedule only for Friday, 9-10:30pm, which was absurd--we're only just hitting our stride by then! Besides getting the Alumni Office to correct the entry, Art had volunteered to open on both Thursday and Saturday--alas, it was completely overcast both Thursday and tonight. He only showed up to take the logbook home for data entry of older entries, but Mike called to remind him that a school group had been scheduled to come and they had not canceled. They didn't show, but several alumni families looking for their kids who were in it did, followed by an enthusiastic couple with whom he and Shianne (who arrived about 10) had a fun and wide-ranging talk (esp. about Steve Squyres' book-signing at the Campus Store the next morning).

[N.B.: The Telrad is fixed for now, via aggressive use of medical tape by Jagadheep.]

Tuesday, June 6, 2006

One guest came by during Shianne's continued cleanup. "I dazzled him with Saturn, the Moon, and Jupiter before he had to go home. Enthusiastic people are so much fun.:-)"

Monday, June 5, 2006

Shianne had begun a multi-day cleanup effort, because "I had a sneaking suspicion that no one else was going to do it anytime soon..." Aside from being repeatedly startled by the compensating binnacle, she practiced on open clusters in Cygnus: NGC 6996, Cr428, Do43, Do10, NGC 6913 (M29), and Be86. ("It seemed like there were so many more!")

Tuesday, May 30, 2006

The high temperatures within the dome the past two days have led to a propensity for stream-of-consciousness log entries--we should consider purchasing a fan. Shianne combined some astronomical/meteorological observing, with Jupiter and Spica hanging over an intense lightning storm in the low southern skies while Castor, Pollux, Mars, Saturn, and the crescent moon formed a group in the west. Shianne's "drive-in omni-theater" experience was ruined only by several spiders and one Mike.

Monday, May 29, 2006

Shianne did some solo observing/practice, looking at the Moon, Jupiter, M57 (Ring Nebula), and M3 (a globular cluster). Jagadheep came by and they played with different eyepieces for Jupiter. With summer having been turned on for Graduation Weekend, the heat made for problems; in particular, the previous night's fix of the Telrad failed and "I found it barely hanging on after I flipped the scope." A permanent solution remains to be found.

Sunday, May 28, 2006

In the afternoon Jagadheep trained Shianne and Art on solar observing, using both the mylar and hydrogen-α filters. Although the Sun is near the minimum of its 11-year activity cycle, we saw not only several sunspots but also no less than five major prominences with exquisite fine structure. (In Shianne's words: "Wow! Solar prominences are even more spectacular than I was imagining, if that's possible.")

In the evening, there was a quiet but steady stream of visitors (final tally, around 35-40) until 1:00am(!), including two ladies in their 70s/80s who were troopers enough to climb the ladder (at least a little ways, with assistance) to look at Jupiter and M13 (Hercules Cluster). The moon was one day past new, so Art began with it: given two little stars near the tip of the terminator, the Moon's motion was visibly obvious in the wide-angle 40mm, and various features could be seen in reflected earthlight. Besides the other usual suspects (i.e., Saturn and Jupiter), we saw and/or showed visitors M65 (a Leo galaxy), M13, M57 (Ring Nebula), NGC 6543 (Cat's Eye Nebula), M19, and M11 in the scope, plus M44 (Beehive Cluster) and M13 in binoculars. (Another Telrad misalignment caused us to stop for a while--turned out the double-sided tape fixing it to the finder scope was failing. We found some more tape in the office and tried a repair.)

We may have also seen what may be the start of a "CAS Summer '06 M13 Time-Trial Competition." At one point Jagadheep loftily declared that "It should never take anyone more than a minute to find M13 in the scope," and was immediately challenged by Shianne to make good on said boast -- he took 84 seconds (the dome had been moved to the appropriate place before timing began).

Saturday, May 27, 2006

While Friday had been a total rain-out, it cleared up just at sunset to permit opening for the first of two special viewing nights for Graduation Weekend. (Well, it had to--it's written in the contract that Graduation Day has to have good weather.) A crowd of 25-30 all showed up at once about 9:30, followed by no one for the rest of the night. While visitors were here we only looked at Saturn and Jupiter; afterwards, Art and Shianne trained under Jagadheep. They found M13 (Hercules Cluster), M92, and M12 (two globular star clusters), but intense ground fog and mist were rolling in and we had to close for fear of moisture damage to the scope. (There was also a misalignment problem with the Telrad finder, which caused a temporary halt to proceedings. Problems would continue over the next few days.)

We also took another peek at Jupiter later in the evening. The Great Red Spot was quite magnificent in the direct center of the planet's disk, and we were able to see another storm (maybe "Red Spot Jr."?) near it. We also observed a transit by one of the moons, including moonrise over the planet.

Wednesday, May 24, 2006

Art opened with the intention of training a bit on some Virgo galaxies, but the clouds came in with their edge rolling by so as to exactly cover Virgo and Leo--even Shianne gave up in discouragement. Art did manage to find the galaxy M59 just after she left, then in frustration looked at Jupiter, just to be able to say he'd seen something. However, he then had a "Duh!" moment of insight, and decided to train on objects in parts of the sky that did not have clouds... He found M57 (Ring Nebula), M56 (a nice globular cluster between Lyra and Cygnus), and M29, a rather pointless open cluster of six stars at the center of the cross of Cygnus. Pon brought his girlfriend by and they took over observing, closing some unknown time later.

Tuesday, May 23, 2006

After a week of clouds, Cornell turned on the weather machine in preparation for graduation and the overcast skies parted. Mike showed some guests a few objects, including Jupiter and the Galilean satellites, and Antares.

Friday, May 19, 2006

Despite clouds on the horizon, we got in a good 2 hours' viewing before they shut us down. About 20 people came in by then; we saw Saturn, Jupiter, M13 (Hercules Cluster), and M104 (Sombrero Galaxy). We could just resolve Castor (multiple star system) in the telescope, while M44 (Beehive Cluster) was viewed through the binoculars. Just as the clouds were moving in for good, some 10-12 people who had squandered the chance to show out-of-town guests the wonders of the universe in order to catch the opening night of The Da Vinci Code showed up, and were told in loving detail all that they had missed...

Friday, May 12, 2006

It's amazing how many people come up here when it's cloudy! We were able to see the Moon and Saturn through the scope, but only briefly. Other than that, we entertained the small but steady stream of visitors by pointing out the stars, one by one, as the clouds allowed. At least it was warm. (Oh, and we're back to Mike's classical stuff again. Apparently he got enough sleep last night.)

After closing due to clouds, Mike and Shianne lingered just long enough for more guests to arrive. Eventually the skies cleared again as more people arrived and we looked at M3 (a globular star cluster) and M13 (Hercules Cluster) through binoculars.

Monday, May 8, 2006

Another special opening, this time at the request of the Kappa Delta sorority, for the "Observatory Date Night". Decorations were put up, tables were moved, and appetizers were provided by two sorority sisters in what was a fun and successful night. The crowd was well behaved and much impressed by Saturn, Jupiter, the Moon, M15 (a globular star cluster), M44 (Beehive Cluster), and of course, our borrowed laser pointer (which works great, by the way). Jupiter was very clear tonight, with the Great Red Spot easily visible.

Also worth noting, Mike, tired from a long night of 'Fortran-ing', gave in to Shianne and allowed her control over the music, resulting in a lot of tacky 80's music emanating from the dome.
For the record and despite being called a "heartless robot," Shianne must say that Mike has horrible taste in music and doesn't know what he's talking about.

Saturday, May 6, 2006

This was a special opening in honor of Astronomy Day. Clear skies and a waxing first-quarter moon provided for fair observations, despite poor seeing. We saw Saturn, Jupiter, the Moon, Comet Schwassmann-Wachmann 3, M5, M55, M56 (three globular star clusters), the International Space Station, and one extremely bright fireball.

Friday, May 5, 2006

Clear if hazy, with a half-moon. About 100 people came through, peaking around 10:00 PM. We looked at the Moon, Saturn, M3 (a globular star cluster), Jupiter, and fragment C of Comet Schwassmann-Wachmann 3. We also looked at M44 (Beehive Cluster), M35 (an open star cluster), and M13 (Hercules Cluster) in binoculars. The treat of the evening, however, was a pass by the International Space Station (observed with the naked eye and binoculars)--most of our visitors were quite excited!

Saturday, April 29, 2006

We viewed a very nice crescent moon; we could see craters on the part of Moon lit by Earthshine (really cool). The CAS members practiced finding deep sky objects (all galaxies unless otherwise stated):

  • Art found M65, M66, M105 (had NGC 3384, NGC 3389 in the same field, as seen through the 40mm wide angle eyepiece)
  • Bernadette found M95, M96
  • Jagadheep found M109, M108, M97 (Owl Nebula)
  • Shianne found M106
  • Jagadheep found M94, M63 (Sunflower Galaxy)
  • Bernadette found M53 (a globular star cluster)
  • Art and Jagadheep found M64 (Black Eye Galaxy)

Shianne found comet Schwassmann-Wachmann 3 in about 15-20 minutes. She challenged Mike to find the comet sooner. Taking up the challenge, he found the comet in 2.5 minutes.

We also viewed numerous objects including M44 (Beehive Cluster), Jupiter, Saturn, and M4 (a globular cluster) through binoculars.

Friday, April 28, 2006

After Krzysztof gave a lecture on star formation, we finally had a scheduled public viewing night. 200-300 people showed up, and some asked some interesting questions. Because of the long lines we only saw the Moon, Saturn, and Jupiter.

Saturday, April 8, 2006

Art asked Mike if he could open for training; it turned into an unofficial open night, being the first clear (if moonlit) night in a while. About 10 visitors came by in twos and threes. We observed Saturn and Jupiter multiple times (including a moonrise over the planet), Mars, M42-43 (Orion Nebula), M35, M36, M37, M38 (four open clusters), M81, M82 (two galaxies), and M4 (a globular star cluster). We also saw two less popular objects: NGC 1907 (an open cluster) and NGC 1931 (a bright nebula). Attempts to find M97 (Owl Nebula), M109, and M108 (two galaxies) failed. Binocular observations included M44 (Beehive Cluster), M3 (a globular cluster), and M13 (Hercules Cluster). We closed down at 3:20.

Monday, March 27, 2006

The Cornell Astronomical Society's 2006 Messier Marathon attempt is described in more detail on its own page.

Sunday, March 26, 2006

An unexpectedly clear, moonless night began too cloudy for a Messier Marathon but provided for some wonderful observing nonetheless. Coming around 11:30, Mike located the galaxies M98, M99, M100, M85, M84, M86, M87, M89, M90, M88, M91, M58, M59, M60, M49, M61, and M104 (Sombrero Galaxy) along with a few NGC objects.

Though Mike knew he should have been resting for tomorrow's attempt at the Marathon, he couldn't resist the brilliant, clear sky. He feels very confident about tomorrow.

Friday, March 24, 2006

The sky was remarkably free of clouds, although the seeing was not that great. Shianne found Saturn, and would have found M51 (Whirlpool Galaxy) had some shady character not taken the star charts. Jennifer practiced finding the Mizar-Alcor double star in Ursa Major and Jupiter. They looked at Jupiter through practically every filter before closing up for the night. The observatory was serene and peaceful, as most of the campus was deserted for spring break.

After spending the early evening entertaining guests in his apartment, Mike [the "shady character"] set out to observe from 1:30am until dawn. With the help of the much missed star charts, he was able to locate the following objects:
M66 (galaxy)
before clouds moved in (stratocumulus)!

Tuesday, March 21, 2006

Michael and Pon came around 2am to view the Virgo Cluster and stayed for 1 hour.

Friday, March 17, 2006

We observed M42 (Orion Nebula), Saturn, NGC 2264 (Christmas Tree Cluster), M51 (Whirlpool Galaxy), and the Moon. It was the start of spring break, so not many students showed up. There were about 25-30 visitors total.

Saturday, March 11, 2006

At 7:00pm Art saw that the sky was (despite national weather forecasts to the contrary) clear and called Mike. The observatory opened at around 8:00pm; two young ladies who been jogging came by almost immediately (the only customers for the evening). We showed them M41 (an open star cluster), M42/43 (Orion Nebula; with the wide-angle eyepiece we could get both in the view and despite a nearly full moon could see an amazing amount of structure in M42), Saturn, M44 (Beehive Cluster) in binoculars, the Perseus Double Cluster, and M34 (an open cluster) before they got too cold.

Mike got M103 before leaving for a flight; Art stayed to train solo. While practicing starhopping to get M52 he got overtaken by clouds. M109 couldn't be seen although he was sure it was in the viewfield. M3 was quite good -- the 40mm wide-angle was quite superior for seeing its details, compared to the regular eyepieces. Art tried to dial in M53 quickly as the clouds moved in for good, but was thwarted. He finally closed at 11:45.

Friday, February 24, 2006

Yet again the clouds arrived within an hour of opening, after an entirely clear day. We had about 8-10 visitors, but were only able to do M42 and Saturn through the scope, although several people were able to see some objects through binoculars: M41 (an open cluster), M44 (Beehive Cluster), M31 (Andromeda Galaxy), and the Double Cluster.

Friday, January 27, 2006

We spent an excellent evening listening to Mozart -- it was his 250th birthday -- but the observing was less reliable: after a clear day, clouds rolled in exactly at sunset to spite us. Nonetheless, there were some good seeing intervals, and we had a very good turnout for the first viewing night of the spring semester. Given the variable viewing quality and bunches of people coming in at times, we bounced back to different objects multiple times. The final tally was M42/43 (Orion Nebula), Saturn, M44 (Beehive Cluster), and Mars. Mike, after most customers left, went for a number of Leo galaxies since they were high up: M95, M105, M96, M65, M66, and NGC 3389. As clouds rolled in for good at 1:20, we ended with the Kyrie Fugue from the Requiem, having done a wide selection of movements/arias/pieces -- piano concertos, string quartets, operas, and symphonies (we did not do the Jupiter Symphony, but Jupiter was not visible anyway).

Monday, January 23, 2006

A wonderfully clear, moonless night allowed for excellent solo observing as classes resumed at Cornell. It is amazing how much more can be seen in the absence of moonlight! The nebulae and galaxies were very obvious, although Mike was unable to see M33 (Triangulum Galaxy) through binoculars. Saturn was particularly lovely, leading Mike to decide that the dome door should remain closed when observing to avoid turbulent seeing.

Other observing targets included M78 (a reflection nebula, very obvious tonight), M42/43 (Orion Nebula), M50 (an open cluster), M1 (Crab Nebula), M48 (another open cluster), M44 (Beehive Cluster), and M97 (Owl Nebula). Mike saw a whole set of extraordinary galaxies: M51 (Whirlpool Galaxy), M101 (Pinwheel Galaxy), M65, M66, NGC 3628, NGC 3605, NGC 3607, NGC 3608, M105, NGC 3389, NGC 3384, M96, and M95.

Saturn and the Beehive Cluster made a magnificent sight in the same field of view through the binoculars. It was overall a fantastic night from 10:00 PM till 2:00 AM. Mike found that the vastness and great scale of space made his suffering on the first day of the semester insignificant and petty in comparison.

Thursday, January 19, 2006

The forecast predicted a clear evening for solo observing, but skies clouded over soon after the observatory opened. The skies began to clear as soon as Krzysztof finished closing up, proving conclusively Mother Nature's malice.

Monday, January 16, 2006

Clear skies and a waning gibbous moon allowed for a cold but decent night of observations, although the first 5 hours of darkness were missed as Mike dined with a friend and his family. Shianne searched for M93 (an open star cluster) and eventually found it. Mike located it again from δ Canis Majoris in a 3-minute timed exercise. Next we observed ο2 Eridani, a remarkable triple star consisting of an orange dwarf, a white dwarf, and a red dwarf. Shianne left early, but Mike observed until 2:30 am and bagged Saturn, M78 (a reflection nebula), NGC 2169, NGC 2158 (two open star clusters), NGC 2392 (Eskimo Nebula), and M35 (another open cluster).

Friday, January 13, 2006

We attempted to have a public viewing for a little while before forecasted clouds moved in, opening at 6:30pm. Art set up M42 (Orion Nebula) for the public; Mike tried for M78 (a reflection nebula) but was foiled by the glare of the full moon. Friday the Thirteenth manifested itself in the form of steadily increasing clouds; we tried to put in a filter for Mars but ran into overcast skies just as three visitors showed up. We closed at 8pm.

Thursday, January 12, 2006

We held another training session, this time with completely clear weather but a nearly full moon. We attempted M33 (Triangulum Galaxy) but failed to find it; we did get M41 (open star cluster) and M79 (globular star cluster). We could see Saturn and the Beehive Cluster (M44) in the same field of view with binoculars. Shianne tried for M93 (another open cluster) without luck. Art tried for galaxies (M108, M109, M51) in binoculars with the same results. A few visitors stopped by briefly at times.

Tuesday, January 10, 2006

After another stretch of cloudy weather we held a short training session, finding the galaxies M81 and M82.

Tuesday, January 3, 2006

After a long stretch of cloudy, rainy, snowy nights, the third night of 2006 provided for 3 hours of clear, moonless skies. The forecast was for mostly cloudy skies, but I, Michael Roman, used my superior forecasting skills to anticipate and take advantage of the clearing. To give credit where credit is due, I simply consulted the GFS and Canadian Clear Sky Clock models for the forecast, which were predicting a clearing since Sunday night. I used the solo opportunity to hunt for galaxies: M81, M82, NGC 2959, M108, M97 (not a galaxy; Owl Nebula), M109, M106, M94, M63 (Sunflower Galaxy)

Saturday, December 17, 2005

This was a training session organized in part to test some new equipment we had received, in particular a new wide-field eyepiece. We looked at M42/M43 (Orion Nebula), the Pleiades, NGC 2264 (Christmas Tree Cluster), the Moon, Saturn, and M41 (an open star cluster). Viewing was not particularly good despite clear skies because the moon was two days after full and because there was plenty of water vapor in the air. On the other hand, the eyepiece performed very well, showing wider parts of the sky than previously possible.

Friday, December 16, 2005

Evening snow gave way to clear skies for about an hour before clouds returned. Having run afoul of Mother Nature, Mike spent his first evening of winter break shoveling unbelievably dense snow under cloudy skies after opening at 10pm. The only two visitors to show up were shown Mars, M42 (Orion Nebula), and the Moon during the brief clearness. Beethoven's birthday was observed with string quartet recordings.

Friday, November 25, 2005

The night was variably cloudy. Most people were away on Thanksgiving break; the only two visitors were friends of Art. We ended up closing early after observing Uranus, Mars, M38, NGC 1907, M36 (three open star clusters), and a most spectacular meteor that was visible through the clouds.

Friday, November 11, 2005

After Krzysztof gave a lecture on near earth objects, we saw the Moon, Mars, M36, M37, M38 (three open star clusters), and M1 (Crab Nebula).

Friday, November 4, 2005

The night was cloudier than expected, but we were still able to get some good observing done. The clouds acted as a natural filter and helped make details on Mars much easier to see. After the weather cleared up we looked at M35, M36, M37, M38 (four open star clusters), and M1 (Crab Nebula).

Saturday, October 29, 2005

About 15-20 visitors came to our public night. The weather started cloudy but cleared up, allowing us to see M13 (Hercules Cluster), Mars, and M31 (Andromeda Galaxy). "After hours" observing also bagged M42 (Orion Nebula), M78 (a reflection nebula), M35 (an open star cluster), Saturn, M44 (Beehive Cluster), M67, and M41 (both open clusters).

Friday, October 21, 2005

Although the night was fairly cloudy/hazy, we had the first public viewing night in nearly a month. A fair number of visitors showed up (about 35). We saw Albireo (colorful double star), Uranus, and Mars despite the difficult weather.

Friday, September 30, 2005

Larry gave a lecture on extraterrestrial life; 37 people attended. The lecture was followed by observing under clear skies. We saw M13 (Hercules Cluster), M57 (Ring Nebula), Mars, and M1 (Crab Nebula).

Friday, September 23, 2005

Mostly clear skies. Viewed M13 (Hercules Cluster), M57 (Ring Nebula), ε Lyrae (quadruple star), Pleiades (through binoculars), and Mars. The night also featured 1 outstanding meteor.

Saturday, September 10, 2005

Our training night was interrupted by an impressive auroral display; we managed to take some pictures (warning: large files). Practice targets included Vega, M2 (globular star cluster), M57 (Ring Nebula), and M72 (a fainter globular cluster).

Friday, September 9, 2005

Yet another clear night in a row! About 60 visitors showed up for our public viewing night, and we treated them to views of M13 (Hercules Cluster), ε Lyrae (a quadruple star), M31 (Andromeda Galaxy), and NGC 7789 (rich star cluster). We finished up with this year's first look at Mars.
