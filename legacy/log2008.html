What we saw

This page will be updated frequently as we hold observing sessions. Editor's note: in April, 2008, CAS acquired a 10"-aperture Dobsonian with computer "push-to" control through SAFC funding. This allows us to have a portable telescope to take to school outreach events and on other club trips. To differentiate observations made with it versus the classic 12"-aperture refractor, we named it "Bob the Dob." Several members had already taken informally to referring to the main telescope as "Irv," since its official title is the Irving Church Porter telescope, after its donor. Thus, most log entries from April 2008 onwards refer to "Bob" and "Irv."

Thursday, August 6, 2009

Pon and Art did the last '09 Cornell Adult University viewing hours—just over a dozen visitors. Targets: Moon, Jupiter + moons, Ring, Owl Cluster, Neptune, M13, M27 (barely!), and M51 (barely!). The solution to the "5th Jovian moon mystery:" there's the arc of three 6th mag. stars just north of Deneb Algedi. Jupiter has been retrograding in front of 45 Cap., the bottom star of that arc, so that several nights it happened to be exactly on the same line as the moons. Given both the moon pollution and Jupiter right next to it, it looked yellow-white, i.e., exactly like another moon...

Friday, Mar. 13, 2009

A quiet night for the beginning of spring break, with about 25-30 visitors including a number of families with small children. It was mostly Brandon on Bob and Art on Irv. Bob targets included Comet Lulin, M42/43, the Eskimo Nebula, the "37" Cluster (NGC 2169), M31/32, the Double Cluster, the carbon star ξ-Cancri, colored double ι-Cancri, plus the truly splendid crescent Venus to start (looked like a 2-day old Moon) and the waning gibbous Moon to end. Brandon also found an unknown open cluster in Casseopeia, which he believed was NGC 365, and installed the new industrial binocs on the stand: targets included M42 and the Beehive. Irv targets included Rigel as a nice obvious double, Pallas, M42/43, M35, M65, M66, and the inevitable Saturn (plus 3 moons incl. Titan). (The Telrad has been reconnected but the display is not working right; to be investigated during some day.)

Tuesday, Mar. 3, 2009

Clear finally, but a killing wind from the northwest. In honor of Square Root Day, Art opened at 7pm for training but no one came by. Irv's clock drive was again dead from the cold. In Bob he only got M31 (no M32), M42, M103, the Owl Cluster, and Venus for focusing before the wind became too much. Closed after barely an hour.

Thursday, Feb. 26, 2009

Very hazy but clear for a little while after sunset, so Art got in ca. 40 min. practice on Bob on early evening Messiers, etc., as marathon practice, and to do more diagnosis on the calibration problem on his Push-To control. He got NGCs 2169/2362/663/457 and Ms 48/50/35/34/42 before conceding—M31 was invisible and the nebulosity of M42 was barely visible. On the other hand, the 2-day old Moon was spectacularly nice before it sank into the approaching clouds, and Venus was an exxxcellent crescent.:)

Tuesday, February 24, 2009

The first clear night in almost two weeks allowed a chance to see Comet Lulin (which, if the orbital elements at heavens-above.com are to be believed, will reach an aphelion of about 4.27 light years(!) in about 23 million years). Very hazy and turbulent atmosphere—difficult to get stars in focus. Art arrived at 7:20pm to check other things while waiting for it to get high enough. The Telrad has been inoperable for a few weeks, making finding startpoints for star-hops somewhat inconvenient, so he got lots of practice during a 2.5 hr. nonstop hop starting at Rigel... Found the asteroid Pallas (first find of it in recent CAS memory), M79, M42/43 and the rest of the Orion Sword objects (NGCs 1973, 1975, 1977, 1980, 1981), M41, and another open cluster Cr140. He actually got Lulin in Bob, plus Saturn (with Titan and another moon) and the lovely triangular open cluster NGC 2362. One person came by and got help finding Lulin in binocs. (Other objects viewed in binocs included M44, M47 at the limits of visibility due to haze, and the α-Persei Group.) Mike arrived about 10:30pm with an Astro grad student, by which time Irv's clock drive had failed due to the cold. After they saw Saturn and Lulin in Bob, Mike got them in Irv for comparison despite the dead clock drive.

Friday, February 13, 2009

About a 50-person evening, with a slow but steady stream of visitors. Extremely blurry at first, but seeing markedly improved later. A variety of targets included: M42/43, M31/32, the Owl Cluster (NGC 457), M45, M46, Venus, the Xmas Tree Cluster (NGC 2264—looked very funky with an OIII filter on, but the nebulosity became distinct), the "37" Cluster (NGC 2169), Saturn, the year's first glimpse at the Leo Trio of galaxies M65/M66/NGC 3628, and the waning gibbous Moon at the end. Early in the evening Don Barry from Astro brought by a visiting researcher from Armenia, who greatly enjoyed getting to actually see things in a telescope; Don also showed us in Bob "the winter Albireo" (later determined to be double star h3945) and a splendid little triangular-shaped open cluster (later determined to be NGC 2362), both in Canis Major.

Friday, February 6, 2009

A clear cold night with waxing gibbous Moon. Nineteen degrees Fahrenheit and some hazy spots didn't scare away the approximately 40 visitors who enjoyed an edge-on Saturn, the Moon, M42, and the Double Cluster. Mike stayed at the end to look for Comet Lulin but was thwarted by Irv's clock drive again failing due to the cold.

Friday, January 30, 2009

On a very cloudy night, we managed to make out the Moon, Venus, M41, and M42. At the start of the evening Art opened for a very enthusiastic group of about 20 elementary school kids and their parents from Moravia, who thought the Moon in Bob was the most awesome thing imaginable:-) and who asked a bunch of great questions afterwards. About 15 more Cornell/Ithaca folks came by before clouds forced us to close.

Monday, January 19, 2009

After several years of trying, Art finally managed to watch an ISS pass in a telescope, using Bob.:-) Irv's clock drive was working, and he managed to get Vesta before the clouds rolled in.

Friday, January 16, 2009

A VERY Cold and Very Hazy night. Although Irv's clock drive was given a lube job the day before, it was still too cold for it. We attempted to see a Venus/Uranus conjunction with Bob but only got Venus: what had looked just what we expected Uranus to look like in fact was merely an optical aberration in the eyepiece. (We realized something was seriously wrong when "Uranus" moved around!!) The very few visitors also got to see M42, M45, M31, NGC 457, and one of the Auriga M-objects (Ed.: some days later determined to have been M36); Mike tried to find Comet Kushida in binocs but failed. The combination of cold, haze, and lack of visitors forced us to abandon dome at 9:45pm despite a "clear" sky.:(

Friday, January 9, 2009

Another "appears to clear in late afternoon then clouds over soon after sunset" night, which dashed a last attempt to do early evening family viewing hours for this holiday season. Irv's clock drive was working again—it apparently needs lubrication, and had seized up due to cold—so Art began opening him up... which caused clouds to roll in within 3 minutes. He and Mike showed the 97% full Moon in Bob to one family group of 5 before giving up. (Earlier, to check seeing, Art had gotten M45 and M42 in binocs. A poor haul for the evening.)

Saturday, January 3, 2009

Art tried to make good on some early evening family viewing hours, but no one came by except one mother and son. He showed them the Moon in Bob and Uranus in Irv, but Irv's clock drive then slowed to a halt despite being newly wound-up!

Friday, January 2, 2009

Given the enthusiastic response to CAS' special early evening opening two years ago for the tenth anniversary of Carl Sagan's death, Art wanted to hold early evening family viewing hours for the three Fridays closest to Christmas—only to have a blizzard on the 19th and rain on the 26th. Today's prospects finally looked excellent, but clouds zoomed in within a half-hour after sunset: he only got to show the Moon in Bob to one family of three (plus Capella, at their daughter's request).

Judging by the number of families who came by before Art could leave, even after it clouded, and the number of people calling the answering machine, there is a LOT of interest in early evening hours.

Sunday, December 28, 2008

A clear late afternoon sky, but plunging temperatures created the infinite line of clouds rolling off the lake too often seen over Fuertes. Before they shut things down, Art was able to see in Bob a Venus-Neptune conjunction (simultaneously visible in Bob's finder), the season's last glimpse of M57, all three of M31, M32, and the barely visible M110 in the 32mm 2-inch; in binocs M45, Jupiter, and Mercury; and in Irv the beautiful double star γ-And., M77, and some barely visible speck at approximately the position of galaxy NGC 1055 vis-à-vis the two guide stars for M77. The highlight was the sighting of Vesta in Irv: the first viewing within recent CAS memory of an asteroid.:-)

Saturday, December 13, 2008

The day after the biggest full Moon of the year (Moon at perigee), there was so much water vapor haze that on this "clear" night, only M31 was visible using the 32mm 2-inch.

Friday, December 5, 2008

A clear but very cold night. The dome got stuck for a few hours but recovered once pushed. While the dome was functioning Irv saw the Moon, the Orion Nebula and M79 (globular in Lepus). Bob balanced between Orion and Andromeda to entertain a light crowd that totaled around 40.

Tuesday, November 25, 2008

Art came by to practice with Bob and try to diagnose the calibration difficulties with him. No solution on the latter; on the former, despite lots of haze, saw a variety of targets over ninety minutes: multiple stars ε-Lyr., the so-called "patriotic triple" ο-Cyg., γ-And.; a number of open clusters including the Double, Owl, Rocking Horse, Ms 103 and 36-38-37, and NGCs 663 and 659; and galaxies M31/32 and NGC 404, next to β-And.—failed to see either M110 or M33. He ended with the Pleiades.

Friday, Nov. 21, 2008

It cleared for a couple of hours after a cloudy day although hazy throughout: we were able to hold viewing hours until 2145. About 20 people came by before the clouds, and another 8 got the cloudy night tour. In Irv we saw Uranus, M57, ε-Lyr., and most of the Pleiades; Bob also got the Pleiades and M57, plus M36, M103, M31, Albireo, and the "patriotic triple" in Cygnus.

Friday, November 7, 2008

A mostly cloudy night, but we were able to show about 15 people the Moon and the Ring Nebula.

Saturday, November 1, 2008

The Russells got Mike to open for them and saw "a number of things, including the first Orion Nebula of the year."

Friday, October 31, 2008

For "Halloween at the Obs" we held extended open hours beginning at 6pm, cut short by clouds at 10pm—cobwebs, bats, carved pumpkins, good scary music, and dry ice were in evidence, as well as candy for children. It turned out to be a slow night for visitors, with only a few families and about 30-40 total visitors. In Irv we got only Jupiter and Neptune, while with Bob we saw M57, M31/32, the Owl and Double Clusters, NGC 752 (open cluster in Triangulum), and ο-Cyg., the so-called "patriotic triple." (Patriotic to U.S. viewers, perhaps: orange, white, and blue stars.) The evening's highlight, however, was clearly the light-saber duels out on the deck, using "real, working" light-sabers courtesy of Darth Aldinger (i.e., Brandon), to appropriate bits of soundtrack.:-)

Friday, October 24, 2008

After a lecture by Stephen on the Large Hadron Collider, we expected nobody due to a heavily clouded night. Instead, a large steady Parent's Weekend crowd prompted a succession of cloudy night tours until we eventually had the confidence to seek out the Ring Nebula (which was clear for about 30 min.). With Bob we looked at Andromeda, M36, M38, the α-Persei group, and ε-Lyr. Overall, a larger crowd than we've seen on some clear nights—wish we'd had more to show them!

Thursday, October 23, 2008

Art opened for training in honor of Avogadro Day, but nobody came. He did some long starhops for Uranus and Neptune for his own practice; he also tried to find Juno but lots of faint stars not on the Uranometria charts were in the vicinity. A Cornell cop came by as he found Uranus, so Art gave him the nickel sky tour and found Jupiter (3 moons, no Red Spot) for him.

Friday, October 17, 2008

A cold night; hazy at first, then got better. In Bob we saw Jupiter (3 moons), the Ring Nebula, M31/32, NGC 7789 (open cluster in Cas., large but with many faint stars), the Double Cluster, ο-Cyg., η-Cas., and a few other unlogged double stars. New member Julia got initial training, finding Vega, ε-Lyr., and the Rocking Horse cluster (NGC 6910). Other Irv targets included Neptune, M13, and the Ring; we ended this ca. 40-visitor evening with Uranus.

Sunday, October 12, 2008

Danny and his father returned to find a somewhat cloudy night. Art came by and found Neptune, which was as much a dot as it's always been. The Moon was also a nice sight, but even it could not withstand the power of the clouds.

Saturday, October 11, 2008

Danny brought his visiting father to see some things. A fantastic night (except for the rather bright Moon), yielding Jupiter, M13, and the Pleiades: cool in both senses of the word.:)

Friday, October 10, 2008

As the American Astronomical Society's Division of Planetary Sciences 40th annual conference convened in Ithaca this weekend, we had the pleasure of welcoming Dr. David Grinspoon as our guest lecturer. An overpacked classroom awaited and enjoyed his talk, "Climate Catastrophes in the Solar System." Afterwards, perfectly clear skies allowed some planetary viewing—Jupiter (with Callisto shadow), Uranus, Neptune, the Moon—as well as M31/32, M57, M45, and ε-Lyr.

Monday, October 6, 2008

A training evening. Moe got initial training, finding Jupiter, M57, and Neptune (with an assist: it looks too star-like). Before Moe arrived, Art checked seeing to find Jupiter "very swimmy," but separation of the ε-Lyr. components was very crisp; and he got open clusters NGC 6633 (Ophiuchus-Aquila border: very big, to be seen only in the wide angle), NGC 6755 (in Aquila: "nothing to write home about"), NGC 6791 (very faint, in Lyra), and M56. After Moe left, Art got Uranus, the galaxy NGC 7585 next to it (at extreme limits of visibility—the Uranometria guide says it's magn. 11.7!), and the galaxy NGC 7331 in Perseus. Being a work night, he reluctantly abandoned a good night after moonset at 2230.

Friday, October 3, 2008

A night shortened by clouds by 2230; about 40-50 visitors, following a lecture by Anthony to about 20. We saw Jupiter, Neptune and the Pegasus galaxy NGC 7331 in Irv, and in Bob got Jupiter (to use Irv for other stuff), M31, and the Double Cluster.

Friday, September 19, 2008

Pon lectured on the entire universe, starting from Fuertes, in 30 min. (He talked fast.:) Viewing followed; a healthy-sized steady crowd all evening, about 100 visitors. High cirrus clouds prevented quality observing, but we attempted to view Jupiter, M57, M31/32, M13, Albireo, ε-Lyr., and the waning gibbous Moon. We were treated to a fine Moon dog to end the night.

Thursday, September 18, 2008

Prof. Nicholson as faculty fellow brought a group of about 15 from Alice Cook House. They enjoyed a tour of Fuertes and the night sky courtesy of Stephen, Mike, and Art, including Jupiter, M13, M57, M31, the Coathanger Cluster, and Albireo. Prof. Nicholson stayed after with the CASers to end with a view of Neptune.

Tuesday, September 16, 2008

An Astro 101 student named Marco interested in joining CAS got initial training and found Jupiter; he wanted to get M28 but flew by without getting it. Stephen passed his key-test, finding Cr324 and M25. Several Astro 105 students who came by got help finding M57, M31, and M13.

Friday, September 5, 2008

The first official CAS meeting of the year introduced many new potential members to the skies at Fuertes. Pon gave a "how to lecture" lecture, then (despite clouds) we observed through about 2300. Irv started at a Jupiter fading in and out of clouds, then moved to M13 and ε-Lyrae. (The dome began to get stuck in the west; we got it moving again but are not 100% confident it's fully unstuck.) Bob looked at Jupiter, M31, NGC 404 (another galaxy in Andromeda), and Albireo. A large number of guests came by tonight to start the semester off right.

Friday, August 22, 2008

The first day of the new school year, and Shianne's final CAS evening/log-entry before leaving for a year in Middle Earth,er,New Zealand. Only a medium-sized crowd showed up. We entertained them with Jupiter and Jupiter entertained us with its Great Red Spot (seen through a green filter) and a transit of Io. High cirrus clouds aside, observing was rather dismal compared to a trip the previous evening out to Mt. Pleasant (whose darker skies allow things like the Lagoon Nebula, M17, to look quite bright compared to Fuertes). We saw three meteors, though—two of them rather slow.
