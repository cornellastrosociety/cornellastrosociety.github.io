Cornell Astronomical Society

What we saw, 2002-2003

All observations were made through the 12" refractor unless otherwise specified.

Monday, June 16, 2003

Despite a 93% illuminated moon, the night was a success. The moon didn't rise above the trees until about 11:15. The dome was opened at 9:30 while it was still fairly bright. Initially, all we could see was Jupiter. The seeing was fair and one of Jupiter's moons was on the verge of disappearing behind the planet. About 10 people showed up and much discussion was had out on the deck. In fact, there were so many questions, the single guide spent most of her time outside talking instead of manning the telescope. There were quite a few questioning minds. :-) Objects seen once the sky darkened were the Ring Nebula and the Dumbell Nebula as well as an aurora.

Sunday, May 18, 2003

The air wasn't very steady, and the seeing was moderate. After showing Saturn and Jupiter, we went galaxy hunting.


  • Leo I - tried to find this for more than half and hour but didn't see anything.
  • M98 - faint edge on galaxy
  • M99
  • M85 - there were two galaxies in the same field! Both quite bright and obvious
  • M100
  • NGC 4383 - a very faint and tiny galaxy at 12.1 magnitude
  • NGC 4379 and 4396 - both were sitting in opposite corners of the field, very faint, at 11.7 and 12.6 magnitude respectively
  • M84

Friday, May 9, 2003

It started overcast at 9pm, but cleared up an hour later. The sky was good for a moonlit night.

Objects seen:
  • Jupiter
  • Saturn
  • The Moon at first quarter
  • Globular Cluster M13
  • Sombrero Galaxy M104
  • Galaxy M87
  • Ring Nebula

Wednesday, May 7, 2003 - Mercury Transit

We hauled 3 Meade 8" scopes to Mount Pleasant just for this half hour long event. It was overcast at about 5am, but by sunrise, the sky turned completely clear in the east. The sun popped out of the horizon clouds at about 6:00, and we went into action. We had 2 solar filters for 3 scopes, so one scope was used for eyepiece projection. Mercury was but a tiny speck near the edge of the sun. A sunspot could be seen in the same field of view. Those present took turns at the scope, following the progress of Mercury through that half hour. Eventually, in a matter of minutes after 6:30am, Mercury went from being a spot at the sun's edge, to a tiny notch out of the sun's disk, finally disappearing altogether. It was a morning well spent!

Monday, March 24, 2003

We finally had a chance to gauge the effectiveness of the lens-cleaning. The Moon was not up, and the sky was fabulous! The faint stars in Cancer and the open cluster in Coma Berenices could be seen with the naked eye. Surprisingly, we saw four visitors who happened to drop by. Here's a list of what we saw (and those we tried but didn't see):

  • Leo I - tried but couldn't see
  • M95
  • M96
  • M105
  • NGC 3384 - quite obvious
  • NGC 3389 - this was barely visible, at 11.9 magnitude - which is good news!
  • NGC 3369 - tried but couldn't see
  • M65 - the distinct north-south elongation was seen
  • M66 - some structure seen
  • NGC 3628 - barely visible, the direction of elongation was obvious
  • NGC 3593 (10.9 magnitude)
  • NGC 3596 (11.2 magnitude)
  • NGC 3655 (11.7 magnitude)
  • M104 (Sombrero Galaxy) - the dark band was quite clear
  • M53 (Globular Cluster in Coma Berenices) - amazing!

And, of course... not forgetting Jupiter and Saturn. Here was our chance to try out the barlow lens that just came in. The seeing wasn't fantastic, so it didn't look much better with the barlow on.

Sunday, March 16, 2003

The second clear night in a row! The air was very steady and this permitted an astonishing amount of detail to be seen on Saturn and Jupiter. A relatively faint moon of Saturn was sighted... the cleaning of the lens might have some noticeable effect after all.

The Eskimo nebula looked very good through the OIII filter, though background stars were dimmed. We looked at M35 and tried to find the other open cluster NGC 2158 nearby, but all that was seen was a loose association of stars. The other objects we saw were M81 and M82. M82 was obvious and looked very thin and extended, more so than before - something we would, again, like to attribute to the lens cleaning. :)

Saturday, March 15, 2003

This was the first clear session in more than a month. The daytime temperature today went into the 50s ... spring is here!

Having just cleaned the refractor lens, we were anxious to look at faint fuzzies through the main scope in spite of the bright gibbous moon. We also set up a Meade LX10, one of the many which are kept in storage.

The first object seen through the main scope was the Orion Nebula, which seem more contrasty. Having not looked through the scope for a long time, it is hard to give an objective evaluation of any optical improvement, especially on a moonlit night. We then tried for the Crab Nebula, with the nebula filter on. This object was barely visible with averted vision. We later directed the scope at M3, M51, and M104. It was amazing that M51 and its companion NGC 5195 were visible. The Sombrero (M104), too, was seen without much difficulty, since it has a relatively bright core. For a moonlit night, this was something!

We tried for more objects on the Meade LX10. However, not all of them could be found. Here's a list of objects that the Meade LX10 actually saw:

  • M42 (Orion Nebula)
  • Jupiter
  • Saturn
  • The Pleiades
  • M3 (Globular Cluster) - unresolved stars
  • M37 (Open cluster)
  • M57 (Ring Nebula)
  • M13 (Globular Cluster)

Friday, February 7, 2003

It was clear from 8pm onwards, contrary to the forecast. The temperature was in the single digits. We looked at regular objects like Jupiter, Saturn and M42. Saturn looked stunning through the 32mm Televue. We could see the bands on Saturn, along with the Cassini division. These details were blurred in higher magnification eyepieces.

The only other noteworthy objects were M81 and M82 - two galaxies in Ursa Major with very different appearances. M81 is the larger of the two, and oriented face-on. M82, the edge-on galaxy, looked more interesting since it appeared very thin and extended.

Monday, November 18, 2002

The Leonids meteor shower was supposed to peak at 11pm on the 18th and 5am on the 19th. Unfortunately, it was cloudy for most of the night. We did catch some meteors at about 11pm-12pm through a break in the clouds in the east. Due to the moon and high clouds, only the brightest stars of Auriga, Orion, and Taurus could be seen. Meteor activity was far from storm level.

The clouds then got thicker and spelt the end to any effective meteor observing. We stayed through the night in the hope that the clouds might at least clear partially, but it didn't, and we decided not to go to the plantations. So we left the observatory at 4:30am in the morning.

Friday November 8, 2002

The first observing session after a long spell of cloudy skies...

  • γ Andromedae, double star, colorful orange-yellow and bluish-green components
  • E.T. cluster, NGC 457
  • Double cluster in Perseus, NGC 884 and NGC 869, open clusters. Very rich, but we couldn't get both to fit into the field of the 55mm wide-field eyepiece.
  • Saturn! The seeing was passable. The Cassini division was discernable, but little more than that was seen.
  • M42, Orion Nebula.
  • M38, open cluster in Auriga.
  • M1, Crab Nebula.
  • NGC 2392, Eskimo Nebula. Tiny starlike haze. A blue tint was reported!

Monday, October 14, 2002 - Fall Break's extended observing session

  • NGC 7789, open cluster, nicknamed the "Magnificent Cluster". Very rich, lots of faint stars.
  • γ Andromedae, double star, beautiful yellow and bluish white components, just like Alberio. It was rather turbulent, but we still managed to resolve the two.
  • M34, open cluster in Perseus. Sparse, counted about 50 stars in the FOV of the 30mm.
  • M31, M32, M110, Andromeda Galaxy and its companions. Very bright and extended. The core managed to fit in the FOV of the 30mm. M32 was tiny but bright. M110 was elusive and faint. It looked more extended than M32.
  • NGC 752, open cluster in Andromeda. Very sparse, more so compared to M34; but it has a slightly higher concentration of stars. Could not even fit into the FOV of the 55mm.
  • NGC 40, planetary nebula in Cepheus. We saw a fuzz around a bright spot... not sure if that was the central star. This object was quite tiny.
  • σ Cassiopeiae, double star. Spent some time trying different eyepieces to see if the bright star was indeed the double. There was significant difficulty in achieving a nice focused image as the shorter focal length eyepieces shows a kind of "fog" around stars. Not very convinced that we saw this double.
  • M1, Crab Nebula in Taurus. Very faint and irregular.
  • M35, open cluster in Gemini. Very rich and wide open cluster. Impressive through the 55mm.
  • M37, open cluster in Auriga. Fits nicely into the FOV of the 55mm. Looks more compact than M35. Noticeable increase in concentration towards the center.
  • M36, open cluster in Auriga. About > 100 stars. Sparse and not very big.
  • M38, open cluster in Auriga. Looks rather uniform and richer than M36, but not as rich as M37.
  • M42, M43, Orion Nebula. An object that never fails to impress. It definitely pays to spend a few minutes scanning around this region. The contrast between the nebulosity and dark area is fantastic. M43 was easily seen as a fainter haze surrounding a bright star. Very impressive.
  • M79, globular cluster in Lepus. A faint and tiny cluster. Stars are not resolved.
  • Saturn and Jupiter. The turbulence was disturbing the image of Saturn quite a bit. The main features were still visible, but not much could be made out on the planet's face. Only two moons of Jupiter were visible.

Friday, September 28, 2002

  • M13, Globular Cluster.
  • Nova 4743 in the "Teaspoon" of Sagittarius, sighted through 16x70 binoculars.
  • NGC 7662, The "Blue Snowball" planetary nebula in Pegasus. Very bright and small in the 30mm eyepiece. Some hint of ring structure observed through the 20mm.
  • NGC 7331, Galaxy in Pegasus.
  • M57, Ring Nebula in Lyra.
  • NGC 7293, Helix Nebula in Aquarius. Very extended, barely perceptible through the Ultrablock filter.
  • M72, a very faint globular cluster in Capricornus. Stars are not resolved.

Friday, September 7, 2002

Northern Lights! We were treated to a spectacular show of aurora tonight. The first signs of the impending aurora storm happened between 8:00-9:00 pm. A faint arc was visible across the northern horizon which cannot be mistaken for light pollution. Not too long after, very intense green rays started appearing in the direction of the Big Dipper, and extended to Hercules and Draco.

A second wave started at about 9:25 pm. Green rays appeared near the constellation of Perseus. This was followed by more activity in the area below the pole star. Some hints of redness could be seen in some parts of the sky. This wave of aurora activity faded considerably by 9:35 pm. The mysterious glow in the northern horizon lingered on for a few hours. Faint rays of northern lights appeared at midnight, but this time it was much more subdued.

We had a great observing session after that impressive light show. The sky was exceptionally clear.
