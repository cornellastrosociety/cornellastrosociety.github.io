Cornell Astronomical Society

CAS Lectures Fall 2005

The CAS Lecture Series is an opportunity for the general public to learn more about astronomy than the weekly observing sessions permit. The series consists of several talks given by CAS members or outside speakers on a variety of topics in astronomy or space exploration. The lectures are held on Fridays throughout the fall and spring semesters in the first floor classroom at Fuertes. Little or no astronomy background is required.

September 30

Looking for Aliens

Larry Klaes

Does life exist beyond our planet Earth? Come see what aliens may look like, how we are searching for them, and what may happen when we do find each other!

October 14

Kepler and the Music of the Spheres

Art Samplaski

In Antiquity, the Middle Ages, and the Renaissance, music and astronomy were connected disciplines, part of the search for aspects of a single universal principle of "harmoniousness." The Renaissance astronomer Johannes Kepler was the last person to look seriously for such a "Grand Unified Theory;" in failing, he laid the foundations for Newtonian mechanics. This talk will look at some of those ancient connections and Kepler's search.

October 28

Origins of the Universe

David Eisler

One of the most fundamental questions in astronomy is how the universe began. Did it just magically appear? Has it always existed? I will give a brief overview of the main theories about the origins of the universe and discuss their implications, from the scientific to the philosophical and even theological.

November 11

Near Earth Objects: Separating Fact from Fiction

Krzysztof Findeisen

It seems you can't pick up the newspaper or turn on the TV without running across a story about a "killer asteroid" endangering civilization. So far all these reports have been false alarms. But why do we keep seeing these stories? Is there really a threat? And, if so, what can we do about it?

December 2

The Big Bang Was Only the Beginning: An Overview of the Largest Explosions in the Universe Today

Richard "Doc" Kinne

The Universe remains an energetic place. Come take a look at some of the latest information on such high energy objects as Gamma Ray Bursters (why they were a military secret years before the astronomers found out about them!), Cataclysmic Variables (stars that just keep on exploding!), and Supernova (the stepchildren of the Big Bang).

It'll make Atom Bombs look like firecrackers!
